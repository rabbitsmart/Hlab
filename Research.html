Research

Human-Robot Interaction(HRI)

Any design must meet the capabilities, limitations, and needs of the people who use it. Industrial designers and UX designers work to improve user experience, prevent injuries, and reduce user error. The interaction between a product and its consumers conveys meaning and invokes emotion. In this sense, all design is emotional, regardless of whether it was intended to be. In his book Emotional Design: Why We Love (or Hate) Everyday Things, Donald Norman emphasizes the role of emotion as we interact with everyday objects. Why are we willing to spend thousands of dollars on Gucci bags and Rolex watches? How will robots respond to human emotions? How can emotion influence our decision-making process and how can design be shaped to influence the emotion in the best possible way?

Emotion and Cognition

The relationship between emotion and cognition has long fascinated researchers and thinkers. In our lab, we investigate different aspects of emotion and cognition, such as the influence of emotion on attention, affective evaluation, visual and auditory emotional processing, and emotional design. We also apply our knowledge of emotion and cognition to the design of everyday things that may be used to promote changes in behavior and affective responses. Our research questions ask: (1) how emotional response of people can be used for learning and conditioning, (2) how the design of automation and technology (i.e., driverless car) can benefit from knowledge of human cognition and emotion, and (3) how individual differences of personality, culture and age reflect interaction of emotion and cognition.

Aging and Interface Design

The graying of populations is a general trend across the globe. By 2050, the majority of people in South Korea are expected to the older than 50. With a rapidly graying population and an ever-present technology interaction, it is clear that human-technology interface should incorporate the needs of older users. In our lab, we investigate the physical, perceptual, cognitive, and affective aspects of aging that are relevant to interface designs. Our research questions ask: (1) whether primary cognitive abilities can predict technology acceptance, (2) how mobile phones can be used to predict and potentially intervene age-related psychological illness such as depression, and (3) what psychological aspects should be considered for the future of interface design.
